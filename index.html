Why is a city sometimes hotter than the countryside around it?

The Phenomenon

The Urban Heat Island Effect

Cities replace natural landscapes with asphalt, concrete and buildings — materials that absorb and store heat during the day, releasing it slowly at night. As a result, urban areas become significantly hotter than their surroundings.

Why Urban Heat
Islands Matter

The Urban Heat Island effect is not only about temperature — it affects health, energy systems and social equity.

HEALTH

Extreme heat is the leading cause of weather-related deaths.

ENERGY

Hotter cities require more air conditioning, increasing energy demand and emissions. Electricity demand for cooling increases 1.5–2% for every 1°C increase in temperature.

Main discovery
Three Thermal Regimes in Milan

The vegetation effect is non-linear. The GPS revealed three distinct physical phenomena based on canopy density.

I · The Albedo Trap
Bare soil heats more than light asphalt
NDVI < 0.18
+0.78°C
Moving from light concrete to dark or bare soil increases temperature. Surface albedo dominates over evapotranspiration when there is insufficient plant mass.
II · Sparse Linear Cooling
Fragmented vegetation fights against asphalt
NDVI 0.18 – 0.60
−0.74 a −0.93°C
Constant but moderate cooling per 0.1 NDVI increment. The thermal mass of cement absorbs most of the benefit from scattered vegetation.
III · Park Microclimate
Continuous canopy collapses temperature
NDVI > 0.60
−2.49°C
Once the continuous canopy threshold is crossed at 100m scale, synergistic evapotranspiration produces a drastic, non-linear cooling effect.
Implication for ForestaMi

Planting scattered trees is not enough. Milan must exceed the critical NDVI 0.45 in continuous 100m blocks for the cooling effect to become exponential. Areas with NDVI 0.35–0.60 are the top investment priority.
